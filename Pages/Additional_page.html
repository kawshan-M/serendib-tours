Colombo

Colombo is Sri Lanka's commercial capital and the country's most populous metropolis. The Brookings Institution estimates that the Colombo metropolitan region has a population of 5.6 million people, with 752,993 people living in the Municipality. It is both the island's financial center and a popular tourist destination. It's on the island's west coast, close to the Greater Colombo area, which includes Sri Jayewardenepura Kotte, Sri Lanka's legislative capital, and Dehiwala-Mount Lavinia. Because Sri Jayewardenepura Kotte is located inside the urban/suburban area of Colombo, it is frequently referred to be the capital. It serves as the administrative capital of the Western Province as well as the district capital of the Colombo District. Colombo is a busy and vibrant city with a blend of modern and traditional cultures, buildings, and monuments.

Colombo was known to ancient traders 2,000 years ago due to its enormous harbor and strategic location along the Eastâ€“West Sea trading routes. Until Sri Lanka was ceded to the British Empire in 1815, it was designated as the island's capital, and its status as such was maintained when the country gained independence in 1948. Colombo was recognized as Sri Lanka's commercial capital in 1978, when administrative functions were shifted to Sri Jayewardenepura Kotte.


Kandy

It is the capital of the Central Province of modern Sri Lanka and the island's second-largest metropolis. It was Sri Lanka's final capital during the reign of the ancient rulers. The city is located in the Kandy plateau, which runs through a region of tropical plantations, primarily tea. Kandy is the capital of the Central Province and serves as both an administrative and religious center. The "Temple of the Tooth Relic," one of the most important places of worship in Buddhism, is located in Kandy. In 1988, UNESCO designated it as a world historic site. Historically, the local Buddhist kings have fought colonial expansion and occupation by the Portuguese, Dutch, and British.

Its geographical location has made it a key transportation hub on the island: Kandy is the gateway to Sri Lanka's Central Highlands, and it can be reached by major highways from all directions. The railway line goes from Colombo, the western coast's seaport, through Kandy to Badulla, the farthest point in the Central Highlands. The main roads from Colombo to Kandy and Kandy to Nuwara Eliya are two of Sri Lanka's most scenic routes, passing through rubber fields and continuous tea plantations. Both highways wind up and around the ring of hills, clawing their way up.


Nuwara-Eliya

Nuwara Eliya is a city in the Central Province's hill country. Its name means "city of light" or "city on the plain (table land). The city, which has a gorgeous environment and a temperate temperature, is the administrative center of the Nuwara Eliya District. It is the most important area for tea production in Sri Lanka, with an elevation of 1,868 meters (6,128 feet). Pidurutalagala, Sri Lanka's tallest peak, towers over the city. Nuwara Eliya is noted for having the coolest climate in Sri Lanka, with a moderate climate.

Samuel Baker, the explorer of Lake Albert and the upper Nile, founded the settlement in 1846. The environment of Nuwara Eliya lent itself to being Ceylon's primary refuge for British civil officials and planters. Little England, or Nuwara Eliya, was a hill country getaway where British colonialists could indulge in their favorite sports of fox hunting, deer hunting, elephant hunting, polo, golf, and cricket.

The Queen's Cottage, General's House, Grand Hotel, Hill Club, St Andrew's Hotel, and Town Post Office are just a few of the structures that have colonial aspects. The colonial style is frequently used to design and decorate new hotels. Visitors to the city can immerse themselves in the nostalgia of bygone eras by visiting the city's landmark structures. Many private residences keep their classic English-style lawns and gardens in good condition.


Galle

Galle is a large city in Sri Lanka, located 119 kilometers from Colombo on the island's southwestern corner. Galle is the capital of Galle District and the provincial headquarters and major city in Sri Lanka's Southern Province.

Before the Portuguese arrived in the 16th century, Galle was known as Gimhathiththa, and it was the island's primary harbor. It was called Qali by Ibn Batuta, a Moroccan Berber Muslim traveler in the 14th century. During the Dutch colonial period in the 18th century, Galle achieved its pinnacle of growth. Galle is the outstanding example of a Portuguese-built fortified city in South and Southeast Asia, demonstrating the confluence of Portuguese architectural styles and native traditions. The Dutch fortified the city significantly in the 17th century, starting in 1649. The Galle fort is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and Asia's largest remaining European-built fortress.

The city's natural port, the National Maritime Museum, St. Mary's Cathedral, constructed by Jesuit monks, one of the island's largest Shiva temples, and Amangalla, the old luxury hotel, are all notable attractions in Galle. On December 26, 2004, the city was wracked by a large tsunami triggered by the 2004 Indian Ocean earthquake, which struck a thousand miles off the coast of Indonesia. Thousands of people were killed in only the city. Galle is home to the Galle International Stadium, which is regarded as one of the world's most beautiful cricket grounds. [3] The ground, which had been badly damaged by the tsunami, was restored, and test matches were resumed on December 18, 2007.

Rumassala in Unawatuna, a massive mound-like hill that provides the eastern defensive barrier to Galle Harbour, is an important natural geographical feature in Galle. The Ramayana, one of the great Hindu epics, is linked to some of the events on this hill, according to local legend. The Gin Ganga, which flows from Gongala Kanda to Gintota, passes through villages such as Neluwa, Nagoda, Baddegama, Thelikada, and Wakwella before reaching the sea. The Wakwella Bridge spans the river at Wakwella.


Trincomalee

Trincomalee, also known as Gokanna/Gokarna, is the administrative capital of the Trincomalee District and a popular tourist destination in Sri Lanka's Eastern Province. Located 237 kilometers north-east of Colombo, 182 kilometers south-east of Jaffna, and 111 kilometers north of Batticaloa, on the east coast of the island, facing the Trincomalee Harbour. With a population of 99,135, it is the largest city in the state. The famed Koneswaram temple is located in Trincomalee city, from which it derived its old Tamil name Thirukonamalai. The Trincomalee railway station, as well as an ancient ferry service to Jaffna and the south side of the harbour at Muttur, are located in Trincomalee.

Trincomalee's known history dates back over two and a half thousand years, beginning with a pre-modern civilian community associated with the Koneswaram temple. It has functioned as an important marine seaport throughout the island's international commerce history with South East Asia, and it is one of Asia's oldest cities. Some of Asia's first medical research took place in Kankuveli, a suburb of Chennai, at the "Agathiyar Thapanam," the Siddhar Tamil medical university founded by Agastya, which helped spread Tamil Tamraparniyan culture across the continent. It was successively the capital of eastern kingdoms of the Vanni nation in the ancient world, developing through the Koneswaram shrine's revenue under the Anuradhapura Monarchy, Pallava Dynasty, Chola Dynasty, Pandyan Dynasty, Vannimai chieftaincies, and the Jaffna kingdom.

Following the Portuguese conquest of the Jaffna kingdom, Trincomalee was transformed into a fortified port town, passing through the hands of the Danish in 1620, the Dutch, the French after a battle during the American Revolutionary War, and the British in 1795 before being absorbed into the British Ceylon state in 1815. The city's architecture is one of the outstanding instances of local and European styles interacting. The city also boasts the island's largest Dutch fort.

The harbor is known for its size and security; unlike any other in the Indian Ocean, it is open to all ships in all weathers. It has been called the "finest harbor in the world" and "the most precious colonial possession on the globe," with the British calling it "the most valuable colonial possession on the planet, as affording our Indian Empire a security that it had not enjoyed from elsewhere." Its beaches at Uppuveli, Salli, and Nilaveli, which are used for temple visits, surfing, scuba diving, fishing, and whale watching, as well as the Kanniya Hot Springs, are popular tourist sites.


Jaffna

Jaffna is the capital city of Sri Lanka's Northern Province. It is the administrative center of the Jaffna District, which is located on the same-named peninsula. Jaffna is Sri Lanka's 12th most populous city, with an estimated population of 88,138 in 2012. Kandarodai, which served as an emporium in the Jaffna peninsula from classical antiquity, is about six miles (9.7 kilometers) from Jaffna. The capital of the four-century-long medieval Jaffna Kingdom was Nallur, a suburb of Jaffna.

It was Sri Lanka's second most populated city after Colombo before to the Sri Lankan Civil War. Insurgent uprisings in the 1980s resulted in considerable damage, the deportation of a portion of the population, and military occupation. Refugees and internally displaced people have been returning to their homes since the conclusion of the civil conflict in 2009, while government and private sector rehabilitation has begun. Jaffna has always been a contentious city. During the Portuguese takeover of the Jaffna peninsula in 1619, it was transformed into a colonial port town, which was later ceded to the Dutch, who then lost it to the British in 1796. In 1986, the rebel Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) controlled Jaffna during the civil war. In 1987, the city was briefly seized by the Indian Peace Keeping Force (IPKF). From 1989 to 1995, the LTTE held the city again, until the Sri Lankan Army reclaimed control.

Sri Lankan Tamils make up the majority of the city's population, with Sri Lankan Moors, Indian Tamils, and other ethnic groups being present prior to the civil conflict. Christians, Muslims, and a small Buddhist minority make up the majority of Sri Lankan Tamils. A variety of educational institutions founded during the colonial and post-colonial periods are located in the city. There are also a number of business establishments, minor industrial units, banks, hotels, and other government organizations in the area. Many historical sites may be found here, including the popular Jaffna library, which was burned down and rebuilt, and the Jaffna fort, which was rebuilt during the Dutch colonial period.
